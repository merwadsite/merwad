Service Design — Diversity, Equity & Inclusion · 2021

Listening wasn't enough.
We designed for it.

"This is a shining example of Service Design for Good — leveraging research, interviewing, facilitation and synthesis skills to drive cultural change." — Gender Equity Committee Director

33

women leaders engaged across peer circles, letter writing, and exit interviews

14

letters written and synthesized into thematic clusters

6

exit interviews conducted with women leaders who had moved on

The Brief

The organization had gender balance through Senior Manager level — and then a cliff. HR's proposed response: listening circles and fireside chats. Well-intentioned, completely blind to the power dynamics in the room. Being asked to articulate years of being dismissed, overlooked, and talked down to — while remaining composed, in front of the leadership structure that would decide their next promotion — wasn't a research method. It was a trap. They said no before it started. That refusal was the first and most important data point.

My Role

Redesigned the methodology in collaboration with a behavioural scientist. We separated cohorts to remove power dynamics, designed a reflective workbook so no one had to go first, and introduced anonymous letter writing so participants do not have to speak or perform composure. I conducted 1:1 interviews with women who had left the specific part of the org to other teams (and flourished) maintaining complete anonymity. Synthesized findings and brought them back to leadership, set up ideation workshops and proposed tactics for specific areas of opportunity for the committee to action.

Outcome

Three opportunity areas across short, medium, and long-term horizons — allyship, bias reduction, and systemic psychological safety. Recognized externally as a model for service design applied to organizational culture change.

Dive into the details

What We Did

Three components, each chosen to reduce exposure and build trust incrementally. Peer reflection circles with separated cohorts. Anonymous letter writing — individually, in pairs, or as a group. And exit interviews with women who had left and been promoted elsewhere — the perspective current employees couldn't fully provide.

I synthesized 14 letters and 6 exit interviews into thematic clusters — working carefully not to interpret but to reflect accurately. Findings went back to participants first to validate, then to the leadership team directly.

Scope and structure — six forum types, 33 participants, designed to reduce exposure at every stage

The reflective workbook — four activities designed so no one had to go first or speak before they were ready

What We Found

The safety problem wasn't incidental — it was structural

Women leaders described a consistent pattern: concerns raised were met with silence, dismissal, or the label of being "too emotional." Psychological safety wasn't a precondition we could assume. It was something that had to be actively designed for.

Flexibility existed on paper, not in practice

"Fake flexibility" — formal policies that permitted adjusted working arrangements, undermined daily by managers whose behaviour signalled that using them carried a cost. What was missing wasn't policy. It was accountability.

The path to director level was opaque by design

Promotion requirements described as vague, shifting, and inconsistently applied. An expectation of "crippling perfectionism" applied to women that didn't apply equally to peers.

The women who left told a more complete story

Every one of the talented women ended up advancing rapidly after moving laterally out of the team rather than up through it — invisible in the org chart but visible the moment you looked for it.

Opportunity areas and action framework presented to leadership.

What I Took From It

The most important design decision on this project happened before any research ran — recognizing that the original format wasn't just suboptimal, it was replicating the problem it was trying to solve. Asking people to be vulnerable in an unsafe container doesn't generate insight. It generates performance, or silence.

The GIC Project: Designing Without Requirements

Branch investment advisors requesting a better GIC rate submitted exceptions through email and Excel. Average turnaround: 4 to 24 hours. In a world where clients make investment decisions in a single conversation, that's not a turnaround — it's a lost sale. Sixty percent of GIC purchases were going through the exception process. Twenty-four percent of requests were duplicates.

The design sprint approach was deliberate. When requirements are unclear and dependencies are in flux, a sprint forces the right conversations faster than a requirements-gathering process ever will. The core challenge wasn't technical — it was translational: the pricing team spoke in language that didn't map cleanly to design concepts. Getting to shared understanding required sitting with the complexity before proposing solutions.

Design iteration — from Excel to purpose-built tool across six weeks.

Analyst flow — the shipped screen sequence for GIC rate exception management.

The RESL Project: The Same Problem, Higher Stakes

RESL rate changes flow from Pricing Strategist to Analyst to Senior Pricing Manager or VP — through multiple Excel documents, email chains, and repeated manual data entry. Current turnaround: 3 to 5 business days. In a mortgage rate environment where competitors move in hours, that's not just inefficiency — it's competitive exposure. The manual process carries real financial risk: one error in 2024 alone resulted in a $200M loss. No audit trail, no standardization, dangerous dependency on the availability of specific people.

The organization had been framing this as a technical upgrade. The VP redirected us because it wasn't. Four workshops: problem statement definition, strategist journey mapping, a continuation session for partners who missed the second, and target state reimagination. Scope expanded mid-project when we discovered the Pricing Strategy team had been left out of the initial brief entirely. We pulled them in.

Pricing Strategist Journey — workshop artifact from the RESL co-design sprint.

What We Designed

Three flows: Strategist, Analyst, Approver. A centralized dashboard replacing the Excel and email chain. Progressive disclosure, reduced cognitive load, intentional checkpoints at high-consequence decision points. The screens consolidate a process distributed across disconnected tools: set business rules, initiate a rate change, input adjustments with alerts for conflicts, generate comparative grids, review and submit for approval. Target state includes conversational guided patterns and potential AI integration.

RESL screen designs — the rate change process consolidated into a centralized dashboard.

What This Work Is Actually About

Both projects are the same argument made twice. The design team's value wasn't in the screens. It was in arriving before the requirements were set, asking whether the right problem was being solved, and staying until the answer was clear.

Service Design — CIBC · 2022

Newcomers didn't need more information.
They needed someone who'd been there.

How research across three cultures and two continents led to a content strategy grounded in lived experience — not institutional messaging.

70

clients, frontline staff, and stakeholders engaged across research and ideation

36

333

The Brief

CIBC wanted to grow market share among newcomers — International Students and Temporary Foreign Workers — by understanding their financial needs from pre-arrival through to permanent residency. I led the Discover phase end to end and co-led the Make phase through to handoff.

My Role

Synthesis of 25 industry reports and 16 internal stakeholder interviews before speaking to a single client. 24 in-depth interviews with newcomers from China (Hong Kong and mainland), India, and the Philippines — deliberately not treating them as interchangeable. 30 frontline staff interviews across 14 banking centres. 4 external subject matter experts. 36 internal newcomers brought in as co-creators. Prototyping and testing, including in-market sessions with prospect clients in India before they'd arrived in Canada.

Outcome

Newcomer Stories greenlit — a content strategy built on real newcomer voices rather than institutional messaging. Three design principles developed for execution.

"You kept newcomers at the centre of everything you did — giving them a real voice as you built insights and solutions." — Senior Director

Dive into the details

What We Did

Research began with a wide net — 25 industry reports and 16 internal stakeholder interviews — to surface assumptions already baked into the bank's newcomer offer before speaking to a single client. We then conducted 24 in-depth interviews with newcomers from China, India, and the Philippines, surfacing how cultural background shaped financial mental models, trust, and the networks people relied on for advice. Frontline staff interviews across 14 banking centres and four external subject matter experts — including the YMCA and academic researchers in financial exclusion — filled in the settlement picture. Thirty-six internal newcomers joined as co-creators in ideation. Two concepts moved into prototyping and testing, including a 333-person survey of prospect clients to refine content positioning.

Banking behaviours across India, China, and the Philippines — why a single newcomer offer couldn't speak to all three.

What We Found

Newcomers trust people who've made the journey, not institutions that describe it

Information about banking in Canada was everywhere. What newcomers couldn't find was someone credible telling them what it was actually like — the things that surprised them, the mistakes they made, what they wished they'd known. Peer experience was the most trusted source of financial guidance, and the bank was entirely absent from that conversation.

The settlement journey is a scramble — and banking is one of many things happening at once

Newcomers arriving in Canada are simultaneously navigating housing, employment, language, social networks, and an entirely unfamiliar financial system. The bank was designing for a rational, sequential decision-maker. The reality was someone managing ten urgent things at once, making banking decisions under pressure with incomplete information.

Cultural context shapes financial behaviour in ways the bank wasn't accounting for

Clients from India, China, and the Philippines brought fundamentally different relationships with money, credit, and institutional trust. A single newcomer offer couldn't speak to all three — and the research made that visible.

Trust is built through community, not campaigns

Newcomers relied heavily on informal networks — friends who'd arrived before them, community groups, social media channels — to validate banking decisions. The opportunity wasn't to shout louder. It was to show up differently.

What we heard — four themes synthesized from 24 client interviews.

The Recommendation

Two concepts emerged from ideation and moved into testing. Newcomer Stories — using real newcomer voices across multiple formats to capture the lived experience of Canadian banking — and an Airport Welcome concept focused on the moment of arrival.

What clients responded to wasn't polished institutional content — it was specificity and recognizability. Newcomer Stories was greenlit. Three design principles guided its execution: lead with the newcomer's immediate experience to signal cultural understanding; focus on daily life and lessons learned rather than product features; and pair self-directed content with clear pathways to a human advisor.

Newcomer Stories prototype — the content strategy concept tested with 36 newcomers and 333 prospect clients.

What I Took From It

The gap between what institutions think people need and what people are actually looking for is almost always a trust gap before it's an information gap. The bank had content. It had products. What it didn't have was credibility in the spaces where newcomers were actually making decisions — the WhatsApp groups, the Reddit threads, the advice from a friend who'd arrived two years earlier. Designing for that reality meant getting out of broadcast mode and into something that felt more like community.

3

The Brief

Grow savings balances consistently and sustainably — without relying on promotional rates that attracted short-term money and created volatility — by understanding what clients actually needed and designing features that could support real behaviour change.

My Role

Led the Make and Do phases — prototype design, test plans, stakeholder engagement, and coaching Deposits partners through the methodology. Involved in the full Discover phase: research planning, participatory materials, interviews, and synthesis. Continued supporting business partners through the build phase to ensure implementation decisions reflected what the research had established.

Outcome

The project shifted the internal conversation from "how do we sell more savings products" to "how do we help clients actually save."

"We want to reduce reliance on mass campaigns and promotions that encourage 'hot money' and volatility. We want to understand what our clients really need." — Internal stakeholder

Dive into the details

What We Did

Three layers. Ethnographic research with 30 clients across 7 cities and 3 provinces — in-home interviews using participatory tools including financial mental maps, life event cards, and savings timelines. Nine banking centre visits and 36 frontline staff interviews to understand how savings conversations were actually happening on the ground. Internal ideation generated nine concepts, which were refined across four rounds of prototyping and testing with 113 clients.

Five insight themes from 30 in-home client interviews across 7 cities and 3 provinces.

What We Found

Savings is emotional, not mathematical

Clients didn't struggle with savings because they lacked discipline or knowledge. They struggled because savings decisions are bound up in identity, anxiety, relationships, and life events that a product feature can't directly address — but can be designed around.

Automation reduces the friction of good intentions

The features that resonated most were ones that removed the need for a deliberate decision each time. Clients wanted to save — they just didn't want to think about it constantly. Automation aligned product behaviour with client intention.

Frontline conversations were inconsistent

Some advisors opened up rich conversations about savings goals and life context. Others defaulted to product features. The gap wasn't motivation — it was tools and time. Frontline needed a better starting point for the conversation, not a better script.

Staying Through the Build

Research findings are easy to erode when they meet implementation constraints. Being in the room through scoping calls, technology requirement sessions, and UX specifications meant we could push back when a decision was drifting away from the client reality we'd spent a year documenting. The handoff isn't the end of design's job on a project like this.

Four rounds of iterative prototype testing with 113 clients — from concept comprehension through to combined flow and notification design.

uLoan at a glance — the product structure the redesign had to work within

The Complexity

A loan application sits at the intersection of competing pressures: customer expectations, regulatory compliance, and stakeholders from product, compliance, legal, and technology who hadn't worked in a human-centred process before. What We Learned About Users

User research was conducted with an external agency across twelve moderated evaluation sessions. My team attended every session. Three user flows, each mapped to a distinct scenario. The format has traveled into insurance work. The practice outlasted the project.

Why Foresight, Why Now

The Advisor Desktop project was focused on the present state — what's broken, what to build first. The Strategic Futures work ran alongside it to ask a different question: what assumptions are we building on, and how confident should we be in them? The two projects were designed to inform each other. One without the other produces either a roadmap with no horizon or a horizon with no roadmap.

The Methodology

Signal gathering across two categories: technology and financial services trends, and socio-economic signals. Sources included ten innovation and financial industry trend reports, HCD journey research, government publications, and academic papers. From the signals we identified two critical uncertainties — the rate and scale of TD's technological adoption, and the degree of socio-economic stability in the environment the bank operates in. Those two axes generated four plausible futures on a 2x2 matrix.

The Four Scenarios

Technological Renaissance

Each scenario was rendered as an assisted onboarding experience — what does a branch interaction look like in this future, what does the advisor need, what does the customer expect? Innovation in large organizations:
Insights into innovation practices in established organizations

The Question

My research partner and I were both working in large, heavily regulated organizations while completing a master's in Strategic Foresight and Innovation. We wanted to understand whether the gap between what our program taught us about innovation and what we were experiencing every day was inevitable — or something that could be designed around.

My Role

My research partner and I started with two separate projects. What pulled us together wasn't structural overlap — it was the conversations we kept having in co-working sessions. We were both facing the same pressure: trying to make innovation work inside large organizations that weren't designed for it. Her context was nuclear power, mine was a bank; yet the frustration was identical. The conversations became more interesting than either project. We combined forces and finished a complete academic project with practical and relevant recommendations neither of us would have reached alone.

Outcome

Four design principles for sustainable innovation in established organizations: clarity of purpose, strategic intent, values enacted, and reflective capacity. That conclusion hasn't left me. Every design practice I've built since has been an attempt to create the conditions the research described.

Dive into the details

Mapping Maturity — innovation process maturity mapped against organizational context axis.

What We Found

The core finding wasn't about process or methodology. It was about values. The gap between what organizations said they believed about innovation and what their actual systems rewarded and punished was wide, and it was structural. Three metaphors captured it:

Our hands are tied

Organizations pulled between the need to adapt and legacy structures that quietly prevent it. Risk tolerance is low, time horizons are mismatched, and projects get resourced only when outcomes can be predicted — which is precisely when innovation stops being innovation.

What does innovation have to do with our work

Innovation practice sandboxed away from the core business, with no shared definition of what it's supposed to deliver and practitioners left to constantly re-justify their existence to the lines of business they're trying to serve.

Not all innovation efforts are created equal

The burden placed on individuals rather than systems. Practitioners expected to deliver results and build organizational capacity simultaneously — carrying personal reputational risk for work that benefits the organization collectively.

Practitioner drawings — participants sketched their own innovation journeys, revealing the gap between ideal process and lived reality.

Why It Still Matters

Sustainable innovation in established organizations requires intervention at the values level — not just new processes or frameworks, but a genuine shift in what gets rewarded, what gets resourced, and what counts as legitimate work. This MRP named the problem. Everything that came after has been an attempt to solve it.

Foresight — OCAD University · APF 1st Place Graduate Group Award · 2018

Precarious growing:
The future of food production

A full-cycle foresight project exploring North American food production to 2050 — from environmental scan through scenario development to a live immersive experience.
In collaboration with Kate Kudelka, Alana Boltwood & Ashley Spiegel — Foresight Studio, MDes Strategic Foresight & Innovation, OCAD University, Toronto

The Brief

The project began with the UN Sustainable Development Goals as a starting point for topic selection. My lens was food growing and production — the supply side of a system under compounding pressure from climate instability, corporate consolidation, declining public trust, and emerging technology that could either democratize or further centralize how food gets grown and distributed. Our primary audience was investors in food growing and distribution — a deliberate choice to make the work applicable in the real world and to force specificity about risk, timing, and opportunity.

My Role

The farming research that underpins the project: collecting signals and trends to form a STEEPV analysis (Society, Technology, Economy, Environment, Politics, and Values). Owned the Discipline scenario — Martial Law Utopia — end to end: the world-building, the narrative, the internal logic. Led the document design across both the APF submission and the full 72-page dossier. Co-built the immersive experiential future, including props, ration bars, uniform design, and performing in the live scenario.

Outcome

First place, Association of Professional Futurists Graduate Group Award, 2018. The methodology established here — signals to trends to scenarios to immersive world — became the template I later adapted for Strategic Futures: Assisted Banking at TD.

Dive into the details

How We Built It

A broad environmental scan across academic sources, current events, popular culture, and mass media — deliberately wide, looking for weak signals alongside established trends. A sensemaking exercise surfaced 16 trends mapped against the STEEPV framework. Those trends became the raw material for scenario development using Jim Dator's Four Generic Images of the Future as scaffold — Growth, Collapse, Discipline, Transformation — forcing exploration of the full range of possibility rather than clustering around the probable.

The four scenarios — each developed from Jim Dator's Generic Images of the Future framework.

The Four Scenarios

Encrypted Food Fight — Growth

Climate change disasters accelerate technological innovation in agriculture, but all breakthroughs are patented and controlled by a single corporation. Food security improves. Food equity collapses.

Survival in a Failed State — Collapse

Climate change and oil depletion destabilize governments and supply chains. Small cooperative communities become the primary unit of food production. Resilience is hyper-local. The formal economy has largely failed.

Martial Law Utopia — Discipline

Intense climate instability triggers political crisis. A newly elected government nationalizes all food production, seizes corporate assets, suspends intellectual property rights, and begins rationing. Austere, utilitarian, surveilled — and by most measures, it works.

Soilution — Transformation

Biotechnology produces a soil additive that allows crops to adapt to climate volatility, restoring traditional agriculture. A government-academic-industry partnership distributes the innovation equitably. Society returns to a culture of growing.

Making It Real: The Immersive Experience

Nutri-Paq, Parsnip-Prune flavour — one of the physical props designed for the immersive future.

Scenario documents provoke intellectual engagement. Immersive experiences provoke something harder — emotional investment in a future that doesn't exist yet. We brought the Martial Law Utopia to life as a live experiential future. Participants arrived as 19-year-old recruits on their first day of Crown Service. Every prop was designed to the internal logic of the world: ration bars, uniforms, scent masks, a planted dissenter.

Crown Service recruitment poster — the world brought to life.

During debrief, participants reported genuine emotional responses: camaraderie with their assigned division, a flicker of national pride at Canada's imagined accomplishments, unease at the surveillance mechanisms threaded through the scenario. You can read about a future. You can also inhabit it — and inhabiting it changes what you do with the information.

On Foresight

Foresight is sensemaking — an attempt to exercise some control over the uncontrollable by holding multiple futures simultaneously. Not prediction. Not a plan. A structured way of sitting with uncertainty.
